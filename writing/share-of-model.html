What Share of Model actually measures

My starting point for making answer-engine visibility concrete without pretending it is more precise than it is.

Search used to be a list of links. A customer typed a query, compared results, opened pages, and made a decision across several surfaces. AI answer engines compress that journey. They summarize the market, name options, explain tradeoffs, and often make a recommendation before the user ever reaches a brand's website. That shift creates a measurement problem for product and growth teams: if discovery happens inside the answer, what exactly should be measured?

I use Share of Model as a practical answer to that problem. At its simplest, Share of Model measures how often a brand, product, or entity appears in model-generated answers for a defined set of prompts. But the useful version goes further. It asks whether the brand is merely mentioned, actively recommended, cited with credible sources, described accurately, and framed with positive or negative sentiment. The metric is less like rank tracking and more like a structured visibility audit.

The first mistake is treating Share of Model as a perfect market-share proxy. It is not. Model answers vary by tool, account context, geography, date, prompt phrasing, retrieval behavior, and source availability. A 50-prompt baseline does not prove demand. It does not prove conversion. It does not prove that the model is right. What it can show is whether the brand has enough machine-readable, citation-worthy public evidence to be considered by answer engines in realistic discovery moments.

That distinction matters because it changes the product question. The question is not "How do we manipulate the model?" The question is "What public evidence would a useful answer need, and have we made that evidence easy to find, trust, and cite?" This is where product judgment enters. A good Share of Model tracker needs a prompt battery that maps to real intents: best-of queries, comparison queries, how-to questions, problem statements, and local or service queries. It needs a frozen scoring rubric before the baseline is run. It needs raw answer capture so later readers can audit the interpretation.

I would measure five things in a first version. Mention asks whether the entity appears at all. Recommendation asks whether the answer actively prefers it or only lists it. Citation quality asks whether the answer points to credible, specific sources. Sentiment asks whether the framing is favorable, neutral, or negative. Specificity asks whether the model gives concrete reasons or vague category language. Together, these dimensions tell a richer story than a single percentage.

The most useful output is not the score itself. It is the delta over time. If a brand publishes better structured data, comparison pages, FAQs, original research, or clearly attributed product information, a monthly rerun can show whether answer engines begin to cite and describe it differently. This turns GEO from a vague aspiration into an operating loop: define prompts, freeze the rubric, capture answers, score consistently, publish the delta, and decide what evidence to improve next.

There are still risks. A team can overfit to prompts. It can chase mentions instead of usefulness. It can confuse model friendliness with customer value. That is why the metric should sit next to human review and business outcomes. If the model recommends a product for the wrong reason, a high mention rate is not success. If citations come from weak or outdated pages, the work is not done. If the answer is technically accurate but not useful to the customer, the content surface still needs product thinking.

My view is that Share of Model will become a normal part of growth measurement, but only if PMs keep it honest. It should be directional, reproducible, and tied to evidence quality. It should make teams ask better questions about public knowledge: what do we want to be known for, what proof exists, where is that proof published, and can a machine cite it without hallucinating? That is the real value of the metric. It turns the answer-engine shift into a product problem that can be scoped, measured, improved, and defended.
